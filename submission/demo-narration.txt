Investor Diligence War Room is built for one painful moment.

An investor, scout, or accelerator operator has messy startup notes. There is a website, a founder claim, maybe a few customer hints, and a lot of uncertainty. They need a useful first-pass memo fast, but they cannot afford a confident AI answer that hides what is still unproven.

This product turns those notes into an evidence-aware diligence packet.

The score is deliberately labeled as an investability hypothesis. It is not investment advice. It is not a final decision. The point is to move faster while keeping the human investor in control.

Here I enter a startup example and build the memo. The app separates evidence receipts from assumptions. That distinction is the core product. If revenue, customer adoption, or market proof is not attached, the app does not pretend it is verified.

Next, the risk map highlights what could break the investment case. Payment willingness, buyer urgency, competition, data quality, and timing are not hidden inside a polished paragraph. They become explicit diligence work.

The next-question panel turns the memo into a call plan. A scout can ask who owns the budget, what workflow is currently manual, what proof shows someone would pay this month, and which claims are evidence versus assumptions.

Then the memo can be exported. That matters because investors already work in memos. The product does not force them into a chat thread. It gives them a structured artifact they can review, edit, and share.

At the bottom, the app shows a sanitized Gemini proof. A real model call happened through Vertex AI. The public proof shows the model, route, HTTP status, and token count, but it does not expose secrets.

The bigger idea is simple: AI can accelerate diligence, but the winning workflow is not blind automation. It is AI speed, evidence discipline, and human approval in one room.
